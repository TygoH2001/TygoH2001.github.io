1) How many of the canal boats currently in use are diesel/fossil fuel driven and how many boats are electrical driven?

Amsterdam wants to ban diesel engines from the centre's canals by 2025 completely. In 2022, approximately 33% of the 7350 registered vessels in Amsterdam were already fully or partly emission-free. That is a total of 2453 boats. Note: hybrid boats are only allowed to use their electric motor inside Amsterdam’s city center and are therefore considered electrically driven for this calculation. [Gemeente Amsterdam, 2022a] 


      # How much is 33% of the 7350 registered vessels?
      
      number_boats = 7350
      percentage_electric = 33
      
      electric_boats = (percentage_electric / 100) * number_boats
      
      print(electric_boats)

Of the recreational boats, mostly privately-owned, only 5 % are emissions-free. Of the 550 commercial vessels, including tourist focused canal boats, 75 % were already emission free in 2020. However, these make up only 7,5 % of all registered boats. [Sterling, 2020]

Try to compare the energy use of the canal boats to some other activities in the city. Use canal boats more or less energy in relation to their carbon footprint compared to these other activities?

It is very difficult to compare the energy use of electric boats to other activities because there is not much data publicly available. Also, the periods of use, purposes of use and boat models differ a lot. We found that the CO2 emissions of canal boats in Amsterdam are included within inland shipping numbers. Inland shipping is responsible for 2% of the CO2 emissions of the Mobility & Transport sector. This is not a significant number compared to road traffic emissions [Hoen & van Wijngaarden, 2019]. Assuming the average person spends 50 hours per year on their boat (with a 50-horsepower motor), they use 175kWh – 236 kWh energy per year [BoaterPal, 2023]. This would produce 0.076 metric tons C02 equivalent.

[BoaterPal, 2023]

To compare, an electric car with in average 20 miles driven per day needs 144 kWh per month, adding up to 1728 kWh per year for average use (python code). This would produce 1584 lbs or 0.7184903 metric tons C02 equivalent.

How many support boats and vehicles are needed for this event?

The yearly Amsterdam city swim attracts around 2500 participants. It is expected that this event will attract at least a similar number. During the Amsterdam City Swim, the safety of swimmers is guaranteed by Royal Netherlands Sea Rescue Institution lifeguards in the water and supervisors on the quay. A first-aid station and a resting point on the water are stationed at every 500 meters [Amsterdam City Swim, 2023]. This seems like a reasonable For a distance of 5 km, we thereby need at least 10 boats for lifeguards and supervisors in this event, plus one for the start and we assume that 3 boats for communication purposes and as a buffer are needed. This adds up to 14 boats

Would you consider it economically feasible?

Looking at the energy use of the needed number of boats (see previous question), we assume that an average security boat has an energy use of 15,5 kWh per hour [Van Mensch et al., 2016]. The security boats will be stationary most of the time but we assume they will be moving for 2 hours on average per day. If the event is lasting one week, we have an energy consumption of 3038 kWh for running the 14 security boats during the event. The price per kWh is about 0,12€/kWh [Van Mensch et al., 2016]. This adds up to energy costs of only 364,56 € for the security boats used during the event. 

 
      #Energy for electric boat motors (amount_boats is extracted from last code snippet)

Would there be any effect on the water quality if there were no canal boats using fossil fuels?

The primary benefits of electric boats are lower carbon emissions, reduced air pollution and reduced water pollution. However, the focus in Amsterdam is on reducing air pollution, so it is hard to find specific data about the impacts of electric boats on water quality. Assuming that no canal boats use fossil fuels in future, that should have a significant positive impact on the water quality in the canals [Hemez et Al., 2020].
